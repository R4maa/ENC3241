# Proposal

**Department**
Environmental Science

**Project Title**
Invisible Waves: Assessing the Physiological and Community Impacts of Infrasound and Low-Frequency Noise (LFN) from Large-Scale Data Centers

**Proposal**

## Background
The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and digital infrastructure has fueled an unprecedented surge in the construction of large-scale, hyper-scale data centers. While these facilities bring digital connectivity, they also introduce a relentless physical byproduct to their host communities: low-frequency noise (LFN) and infrasound. To prevent massive server banks from overheating, data centers rely on colossal industrial cooling systems, including high-velocity exhaust fans, liquid chillers, and backup diesel generators that operate continuously. These systems generate significant LFN (typically in the 20 to 100 Hz range) and infrasound (below 20 Hz) (Alves et al., 2020). Unlike high-frequency noise, which is easily absorbed by trees and walls, LFN waves travel vast distances with minimal energy loss and easily penetrate standard residential building materials, often matching the resonant frequency of rooms and causing physical vibrations (EESI, 2023). Prolonged exposure to LFN and infrasound has been increasingly linked by the World Health Organization and environmental researchers to an overactive hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis stress response. Documented impacts on communities include severe sleep disruption, psychological fatigue, hypertension, vertigo, and cognitive impairment (Baliatsas et al., 2016). Despite this, current municipal noise ordinances almost exclusively measure sound on the dBA scale (which filters out low frequencies to mimic human hearing) rather than the dBC scale, leaving communities entirely unprotected from this invisible acoustic pollution.

---

## Methodology
This project will utilize a mixed-methods approach combining acoustic field measurements with longitudinal community health surveys. We will select two communities located within 1.5 miles of newly operational, hyper-scale data centers (e.g., in "Data Center Alley" in Loudoun County, Virginia, or Granbury, Texas).

* **1. Acoustic Monitoring:** We will deploy specialized low-frequency acoustic data loggers capable of measuring on the dBC and unweighted scales (measuring down to 10 Hz). Monitors will be placed at 20 participating residential properties at varying distance intervals (0.25 miles, 0.5 miles, 1 mile, and 1.5 miles) from the facilities for a continuous 60-day period to capture baseline LFN and infrasound exposure.
* **2. Community Health Surveys:** 40 adult residents across the monitored properties will complete a validated, weekly digital survey (using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index and the Perceived Stress Scale) to report on sleep disturbances, perceived vibrations, fatigue, and general well-being.
* **3. Data Correlation:** Quantitative acoustic data will be mapped against the qualitative survey responses to determine the correlation between LFN intensity, distance from the facility, and reported physiological symptoms.

---

## Anticipated Outcomes
The research will likely yield empirical evidence demonstrating that indoor LFN and infrasound exposure remains dangerously high even at distances traditionally deemed "safe" by local zoning laws. We anticipate finding a strong, positive correlation between the documented intensity of dBC/infrasound levels and elevated reports of insomnia, stress, and annoyance among nearby residents.

---

## Significance
Current regulatory frameworks for industrial noise are technologically outdated, relying on dBA-weighted measurements that effectively ignore the unique acoustic signature of data centers. This project will provide empirical, actionable data proving that digital infrastructure has tangible, unmeasured physical consequences on human biology.

---

## Intended Audience
This research is intended for local policymakers, urban planners, public health officials, environmental justice advocates, and acoustic engineers within the tech industry.

---

## Project Benefits
This work will provide communities with the scientific backing needed to advocate for modernized noise ordinances that include strict dBC and infrasound limits. Additionally, it will offer the tech industry vital insights into the community impact of their facilities, incentivizing the adoption of quieter, more advanced cooling technologies (such as liquid immersion cooling) and more responsible site-planning.
